# Scenario 1: Journey of a Product — Factory to Consumer

## Context

You've just joined the analytics team at Frito-Lay (a PepsiCo subsidiary), supporting the snacks division. On your first day, your manager pulls you aside and says:

> "Before we dive into dashboards and models, you need to understand how our products actually reach consumers. Trace the path of a **$4.99 bag of Doritos Cool Ranch 9oz** from our factory in Plano, Texas to a consumer's home in Austin — bought at a 7-Eleven on Guadalupe Street near UT campus."

This scenario is your first assignment. Why start here? Because every data point you'll work with later — store sales, SKU performance, distribution reach — is generated by this physical journey. If you don't understand the journey, the data will be just numbers.

---

## The CPG Value Chain

A product in the CPG industry passes through **different paths** before reaching the consumer, depending on the retail channel. Let's walk through each path with our Doritos Cool Ranch bag.

### The Three Distribution Paths

```
PATH 1 — Independent Retailers (NOT our focus):
Factory → Wholesale Distributor → Retail Distributor → Local Store → Consumer

PATH 2 — Small-Format Chains (OUR TARGET):
Factory → Regional Distribution Center → Store → Consumer
              (Direct Store Delivery - DSD)

PATH 3 — Large-Format Chains (NOT our focus):
Factory → Retailer DC → Store → Consumer
```

---

## Path 1: Independent Retailers — The Traditional Route

### Node 1: Manufacturer (PepsiCo/Frito-Lay, Plano Factory)

The factory produces the chips, packages them, and ships them in bulk — typically in cases of 12-24 bags. The **factory gate price** (also called ex-factory price) for our $4.99 MSRP bag might be around **$2.50-2.75**.

Key facts:
- Manufacturers don't sell directly to millions of retailers — it's logistically impossible
- They rely on a distribution network to achieve **reach**
- The US has ~4 million retail outlets; no single company can service them all directly

### Node 2: Wholesale Distributor

The wholesale distributor is a **warehousing and logistics partner**. They:

- Receive bulk stock from the factory
- Store it in regional warehouses (e.g., one in Dallas covering Texas)
- Forward stock to retail distributors based on demand signals
- **Take ownership** of the goods and earn a markup (typically 3-8% of invoice value)

Think of them as the "hub" of the CPG distribution network. They buy from manufacturers and sell to smaller distributors or retailers.

**Inventory held:** 15-30 days of regional demand

### Node 3: Retail Distributor

This is where the real action happens. The retail distributor:

- **Buys stock** from the wholesale distributor at a **distributor cost** (~$2.75-3.00 for our bag)
- Maintains a team of **sales representatives** who visit retail stores daily
- Extends **credit** to retailers (7-30 day payment terms)
- Handles **returns and replacements**
- Earns a **margin of 8-12%** on MSRP

A typical urban distributor in Austin might service **200-500 retail outlets** with a team of 5-8 sales reps.

**Inventory held:** 7-15 days

### Node 4: Retailer (Independent Store)

The retailer is the final business node before the consumer:

#### Independent Retailers — Local Grocery/Corner Stores
- Small, independently owned neighborhood shops
- ~2.5 million outlets in the US
- Typically 1,000-5,000 sq ft
- Owner-operated, family businesses
- Buy on credit from distributors
- Earn **15-25% margin** on MSRP
- Our Doritos bag sells at MSRP ($4.99) or sometimes at a small discount

### Node 5: Consumer

The end of the journey. The consumer picks up the Doritos bag from a shelf at a local corner store near home.

## Path 2: Small-Format Chains — Direct Store Delivery (OUR TARGET)

This is the path our recommendation engine focuses on.

### Node 1: Manufacturer (PepsiCo/Frito-Lay, Plano Factory)

Frito-Lay produces the chips at their manufacturing facility. For our $4.99 MSRP Doritos Cool Ranch 9oz bag, the ex-factory price is around **$2.75-3.00**.

Key facts:
- Frito-Lay is one of the largest **Direct Store Delivery (DSD)** operators in the US
- They bypass traditional distributors and deliver directly to chain stores
- This gives them control over shelf placement, freshness, and inventory

### Node 2: Frito-Lay Regional Distribution Center

Instead of using third-party wholesale distributors, Frito-Lay operates their own regional DCs:

- The **Dallas/Fort Worth DC** covers all of Texas
- Receives bulk stock from multiple Frito-Lay factories
- Sorts and consolidates orders by store
- Loads Frito-Lay delivery trucks with mixed-SKU loads for each store

**Inventory held:** 5-10 days of regional demand

### Node 3: Frito-Lay Delivery Truck (Direct Store Delivery)

This is where DSD differs from traditional distribution:

- A Frito-Lay driver (who is also a merchandiser) delivers directly to each 7-Eleven store
- The driver unloads the truck, stocks the shelves, and rotates product (FIFO — first in, first out)
- They check **planogram compliance** — making sure each SKU is in its assigned slot
- They collect **unsold/returned product** and process credits on the spot
- Payment is handled separately through the chain's accounts payable system

**Key DSD advantages:**
- **Fresher product** — direct from factory to shelf in 2-5 days
- **Better shelf presentation** — Frito-Lay controls the planogram
- **Real-time data** — driver reports sales, OOS, and inventory daily
- **No distributor margin** — Frito-Lay keeps the 8-12% that would go to a distributor

### Node 4: Retailer (7-Eleven, Circle K, Dollar General, Wawa)

The small-format chain store:

- **Store size:** 2,500-3,500 sq ft
- **SKUs stocked:** 50-150 snack SKUs from Frito-Lay
- **Monthly turnover:** $150,000-$500,000
- **How they buy:** Hybrid model — HQ sets assortment guidelines, franchise operator has 10-20% autonomy
- **Payment terms:** 15-45 days
- **Pricing:** At or slightly below MSRP
- **Earns 20-30% margin** on MSRP
- Our Doritos bag sells at $4.49-4.99

### Node 5: Consumer

The consumer picks up the Doritos bag from a 7-Eleven shelf — perhaps during a late-night study session near UT campus or on the way home from work at The Domain office park.

## Path 3: Large-Format Chains — The Corporate Route

### Node 1: Manufacturer (PepsiCo/Frito-Lay)

Frito-Lay's **Key Account Manager (KAM)** negotiates directly with Walmart's central buying team in Bentonville, Arkansas.

### Node 2: Walmart Regional Distribution Center

Walmart operates their own DCs. Frito-Lay ships truckloads to Walmart's DC, which then distributes to individual stores.

### Node 3: Walmart Store

The store receives product from Walmart's DC (not from Frito-Lay directly). Orders are automated based on POS data.

### Node 4: Consumer

The consumer buys Doritos at Walmart, often at a discounted price ($3.99-4.49 vs. $4.99 MSRP).

## Margin Waterfall: Where Does the $4.99 Go?

Let's trace the money for one Doritos Cool Ranch 9oz bag through each channel:

### Before we trace the money: What is Trade Spend?

You might expect that if Frito-Lay sells a bag at $2.50 factory gate price, they keep $2.50. They don't. In CPG, the manufacturer **pays money back** to the channel to maintain distribution. This is called **trade spend** and it's one of the biggest costs in the industry (typically 15-20% of gross revenue). It includes:

- **Volume rebates** — "Buy 10,000 cases this quarter, get 5% back"
- **Slotting fees** — Payments to retailers for shelf space and planogram placement
- **Promotional discounts** — Frito-Lay funds "Buy 2 for $7" deals at the store
- **Damaged/expired credits** — Taking back unsold bags and issuing credit to the channel
- **Co-op advertising** — Funding retailer flyers and in-store displays that feature Doritos

For our $4.99 bag, Frito-Lay invoices the wholesaler at $2.50 but pays back ~$0.40 per bag in trade spend. So their **company realization** — what they actually keep — is ~$2.10.

### Independent Retailer Channel

| Node | Price | What's happening | Margin | % of MSRP |
|------|-------|-----------------|--------|-----------|
| Factory gate price | $2.50 | Frito-Lay ships from factory | — | 50% |
| + Wholesale markup | $2.70 | Wholesaler adds warehousing & logistics markup | $0.20 | 4% |
| + Freight to distributor | $3.00 | Transport & handling to reach retail distributor | $0.30 | 6% |
| + Distributor costs & margin | $3.99 | Delivery fleet, sales reps, credit, returns ($0.49 operating costs + $0.50 profit margin) | $0.50 (~10%) | 10% |
| + Retailer margin | $4.99 | Store operations, shelf space, staff | $1.00 (~20%) | 20% |
| **Consumer pays (MSRP)** | **$4.99** | | | **100%** |
| Sales tax | ~$0.40 | | | ~8% |
| **Company realization** | **~$2.10** | Factory gate ($2.50) minus trade spend (~$0.40) | | **~42%** |

### Small-Format Chain Channel (DSD)

| Node | Price | What's happening | Margin | % of MSRP |
|------|-------|-----------------|--------|-----------|
| Factory gate price | $2.75 | Frito-Lay ships from factory to regional DC | — | 55% |
| + Frito-Lay DC operations | $3.25 | Warehousing, sorting, consolidating mixed-SKU loads | $0.50 | 10% |
| + DSD delivery & merchandising | $3.75 | Driver delivers to store, stocks shelves, checks planogram, collects returns | $0.50 | 10% |
| + Retailer margin | $4.99 | Store operations, shelf space (25% of MSRP) | $1.24 (~25%) | 25% |
| **Consumer pays (MSRP)** | **$4.99** | | | **100%** |
| Sales tax | ~$0.40 | | | ~8% |
| **Company realization** | **~$2.25** | Factory gate ($2.75) minus trade spend (~$0.50) | | **~45%** |

### Large-Format Chain Channel

| Node | Price | What's happening | Margin | % of MSRP |
|------|-------|-----------------|--------|-----------|
| Factory gate price | $3.25 | Frito-Lay sells directly to Walmart's central buying team at negotiated bulk price | — | 65% |
| + Walmart DC cost | $3.50 | Walmart distributes from their own DC to stores | $0.25 | 5% |
| + Retailer margin | $4.49 | Walmart sells at ~10% below MSRP to drive foot traffic and volume | $0.99 (~20%) | 20% |
| **Consumer pays (discounted)** | **$4.49** | ~10% below MSRP of $4.99 | | **90%** |
| Sales tax | ~$0.36 | | | ~7% |
| **Company realization** | **~$3.00** | Factory gate ($3.25) minus trade spend (~$0.25) | | **~60%** |

> **Key insight:** Frito-Lay realizes the **highest margin per unit** through large-format chains (60%) because they eliminate distributor margins. However, large-format chains demand deep discounts and have their own analytics teams. Small-format chains (DSD) offer a **sweet spot**: good margins (45%) + rich data + field rep influence for recommendations.

## Why Distribution Reach > Advertising

Here's a number that surprises most people:

> **A product available in 500,000 stores will almost always outsell a heavily advertised product available in only 50,000 stores.**

This is the fundamental truth of CPG: **availability drives sales more than awareness**. A consumer who wants Doritos Cool Ranch but finds only Lay's Classic on the shelf will buy Lay's. The sale is lost forever — they won't drive to another store.

This is exactly why our recommendation engine matters. By recommending the **right SKUs to the right stores**, we're increasing **effective distribution** — making sure the products most likely to sell are actually present on shelves.

---

## Why Our Recommendation Engine Targets Small-Format Chains

| Factor | Independent | Small-Format Chain (Our Target) | Large-Format Chain |
|--------|-------------|--------------------------------|-------------------|
| **Data availability** | Poor — paper invoices | Rich — POS, loyalty, real-time | Excellent — full digital |
| **Decision-maker access** | High — owner decides | Medium-High — franchise + field rep | Low — central buying team |
| **Assortment criticality** | Medium — 15-50 SKUs | High — 50-150 SKUs, limited space | Low — 80-150 SKUs, plenty of space |
| **Recommendation influence** | High — sales rep pitches | Medium-High — field merchandiser + data | Low — algorithms decide |
| **Scale** | 2.5M stores | 80,000+ stores | 150,000 stores |
| **Data science techniques possible** | Basic — order history only | Advanced — POS, basket analysis, forecasting | Advanced — but internal teams handle it |

**The sweet spot:** Small-format chains have **rich data** (enabling advanced techniques) + **human decision-makers** (who can act on recommendations) + **assortment constraints** (making recommendations valuable).

## Key Terms Glossary

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **CPG** | Consumer Packaged Goods — fast-moving products sold through retail (snacks, beverages, detergent) |
| **DSD (Direct Store Delivery)** | Manufacturer delivers directly to retail store, bypassing distributors |
| **Wholesale Distributor** | Warehousing/logistics partner who buys from manufacturers and sells to retail distributors |
| **Retail Distributor** | Buys stock from wholesale distributors and services retail outlets through sales reps |
| **Independent Retailers** | Traditionally owned retail stores (local grocery, corner stores) |
| **Small-Format Chains** | Franchised convenience chains — 7-Eleven, Circle K, Dollar General, Wawa |
| **Large-Format Chains** | Organized retail chains — Walmart, Target, Kroger, CVS, Walgreens |
| **MSRP** | Manufacturer's Suggested Retail Price — the recommended price for the product |
| **Ex-factory price** | The price at which goods leave the factory (before channel margins) |
| **Channel margin** | The percentage earned by each intermediary in the distribution chain |
| **Planogram** | Visual diagram showing exactly where each product goes on shelves |
| **Regional DC** | Regional Distribution Center — warehouse serving a geographic area |
